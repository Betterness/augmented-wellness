*Augmented Wellness — a podcast about how AI, health data, and human innovation

## Who is Martin

In the host's introduction, Martin is described as "a dear friend, a mentor,
zero founder, investor, father of seven, avid cyclist," and "most recently
founder of [Certuma], a company building at the center of AI and healthcare."

In his own words, Martin says nothing in his childhood suggested he would
"become an entrepreneur," "build six unicorns over 35 years," or "have seven
children." He grew up in Argentina during what he calls "a civil war between
the guerrilla movement and the military," an upbringing he describes as "in the
middle of death and fear" — his father was kidnapped by the left and a cousin
was murdered by the right. He arrived in New York in the late 1970s ("the taxi
driver New York City"), studied premed at NYU, and describes his first 25 years
as hard: his parents divorced and his father died when he was 22, leaving him to
support his mother and grandmother. He frames the rest — "all these happy Martin
Varsavsky stories" — as everything after.

## The arc of the conversation

The episode moves deliberately from **how Martin builds the future** to **how he
lives a full, active, curious life while doing it** — the two sides the host
says up front he wants to understand. It travels from origin story → the
contradiction of the bank that rejected him then funded him → real estate as the
seed capital he used to escape into tech → a life spread across many homes →
GLP-1s and what he takes → cycling and self-quantification → the app he coded for
himself → Certuma and an AI raised with no co-founder but the model itself →
the agentic-doctor products → and finally "Pedro," his disclosed AI persona on X.
It closes with a live demo of a locally trained Varsavsky voice agent (which, in
classic demo fashion, took a couple of tries to come alive).

## What we discussed

A fuller walk through the conversation, in Martin's own arc. Everything below is
grounded in what was actually said in the episode; short quotes are verbatim, and
anything restated is marked as paraphrase.

### A hard first 25 years — Argentina and 1970s–80s New York

Martin opens by pushing back on his own reputation. *Paraphrase:* nothing in his
childhood suggested he'd become an entrepreneur or build six unicorns over 35
years — "people know me about sort of doing well and prospering," but the
beginning "was actually a very difficult life." He grew up in Argentina during
what he calls "a civil war between the guerrilla movement and the military," and
refuses the tidy good-side/bad-side framing: "I just think there were bad people
on all sides." It got personal — "my dad was kidnapped by the left, my cousin was
murdered by the right" — an upbringing "in the middle of death and fear." His one
silver lining is a recalibrated relationship with risk: "risk is relative when
you grew up facing death."

Then came New York "of the late 70s, which was the taxi driver New York City."
*Paraphrase:* he traded the danger of a war for the danger of just walking around
— mugged twice, his car and apartment broken into — while studying premed at NYU.
He's blunt that the hardship ran to age 25: his parents divorced, his father died
when he was 22, he had no money, and he had to support his mother and grandmother.
"All these happy Martin Varsavsky stories are the rest of my life, but certainly
not the first 25 years."

### The bank that rejected him — then handed him a $9M loan

The hinge anecdote: "The same bank, Midland Bank rejected me for a job and gave me
a 9 million dollar loan that was my first breakthrough in life," for "a loft
building that I was developing while I was a student." *Paraphrase:* he reads the
job rejection as a read on his younger self — "so defiant and difficult" — and
recounts the classic interview question. Asked where he saw himself in five years,
he answered: "well, at least as your boss." That attitude sank the job interview;
the same attitude, applied to debt rather than a salary, worked.

### Real estate, pre-money valuations, and rezoning Tribeca lofts

"Real estate is what got me started, what I made my first million with real
estate," he says — surprising, he notes, "because I'm a tech entrepreneur." Broke
after his father's death, he asked himself "what is the lowest hanging fruit to
make money?" and took an entrepreneurship class that taught him "the most
important thing I ever learned at university… pre-money valuations." His reaction:
"this is insane, this is like printing money."

He founded **Urban Capital Corporation** and, between ages 19 and 25, developed
"half a million square feet of lofts in Manhattan." *Paraphrase:* the playbook
came from his downtown squatter friends and the city's zoning. Manhattan had zoned
old buildings industrial to protect manufacturing, so he gave equity to **Leonard
Kahn** — a former city "commissioner for leasing" — and started "a movement to
legalize the illegal squatting lofts." The result was a rezoning at **11 Beach
Street in Tribeca** from industrial to office/residential: "when the building was
re-zoned, then the value went up like five times." That one building he bought
"for like six million" and "ended up selling for 62 million." But the asset class
never moved him: "I personally didn't like real estate, I loved tech and I love
science." Real estate "only gave me my first money to do anything else" — he could
have "retired at 25" and instead went into biotech and tech.

### A full life across many homes

Martin reframes his later property buying as the opposite of his developer days:
"This was more of a random activity that I had more to do with emotions than with
rational thinking." *Paraphrase:* he came to Miami around 2009 when prices were
roughly 80% below today and "they were giving apartments away," bought in **José
Ignacio, Uruguay** when it was "worth about a farm for 14,000," and chose
**Sagaponack** in the Hamptons — "a farm from the 1800s with silos" — over the
fashionable Southampton. He says he and his wife have "around 12" homes, bought
"just because I thought they were beautiful, not because I was thinking… I'm going
to make money," with the **Tulum hotel (2016)** the one explicit exception ("more
of a business thing"). Seven children, three still living with them, school in
Madrid, and a family arrangement that they all come to the States on vacations.
He loves living in Spain but says there's "something exhausting about America that
tires me out" when he isn't working — so he works in the US and recovers in
Europe, Argentina, or Uruguay.

### GLP-1s and Mounjaro — and where the science stops

On health, Martin draws a line: "there's science and there's pseudoscience." He's
taken **Mounjaro** "for two years" "even though my weight is the same," because he
believes "it has effects beyond weight." *Paraphrase:* he used to fight to keep
his weight — on the scale every day, measuring what he ate — and now weighs the
same "without effort." The line he keeps returning to: "I don't have the food
noise, and I don't have to think about food as I used to." He gives his own
mechanistic framing — GLP-1 signals satiation across "your pancreas and your gut
and your brain," GIP touches "your gut and your liver and your brain" and the
"conversion of glucose into fat" — and flags the caution that if you take it and
aren't overweight, "it doesn't eat in your muscles, so I also take creatine," plus
heavy training: "it's not the [Mounjaro] alone." On everything else he's
deliberately conservative: with other peptides "I haven't done anything because I
don't think the science is there… I don't like to experiment myself in things that
I don't understand or that are highly speculative."

### Cycling at 66 — ~14 hours a week, no fractures

Asked how he fits it in, Martin credits geography: living in Spain and working in
America means "when I go cycling every morning, people are asleep in the States,"
as if he woke at 4 a.m. stateside. It's pure habit now — "I wake up and I have to
do it… I don't even think about it." He marvels that "at 66, I don't have any
pains or aches or injuries," which he ties to durability: "I had maybe four
accidents in my life. But I never broke anything… I had maybe 120 stitches… but
not fractures." His risk-management is concrete — ride "where there are very few
cars," and alternate "mountain and road and gravel." One aside that lands with the
host: he "cycle[s] significantly more slowly if I'm listening to a podcast or an
audiobook" versus music — "if your brain is working, somehow it sucks up the
energy."

### Building his own app — "Stravar" and "U versus U"

Martin built his own cycling app on top of Strava: "instead of Strava is called
**Stravar** because of Varsavsky," "a private app I haven't put it in the app
store." *Paraphrase:* he still logs to Strava to keep his record since 2010, but
Stravar gives him metrics Strava doesn't. He calls out climbing rate — "meters per
hour… the concept of your momentum while you're climbing" — and real **watts**
"with sensors on the bike," noting "Strava can guess the watts, but you don't get
the watts." The centerpiece is the metric he named **"U versus U."** Having gone
up the same mountain "maybe 200 times," he coded his "performance on every climb,"
so he can see "my climbing better than my average… worse than my average. That's
the U versus U… where I'm ranking against myself since 2010." The point isn't
medals — over 16 years most people can't beat their younger selves at the same
segment — it's that he hasn't "gone down the rate at which I climb these
mountains." On the build itself: "I built it… with AI on Xcode and Swift" back
"when… vibe coding was not called vibe coding."

### Data as motivation — Garmin over Apple, and a radar that talks

His relationship to data is unapologetically motivational. He switched from the
**Apple Watch** to a **Garmin Venue 3** "because it broadcasts my heart rate" —
*paraphrase:* Apple "tries to keep you in the Apple ecosystem," while Garmin
broadcasts into both his own app and Strava, giving him "more flexibility." He
watches watts, heart rate, climbing meters-per-hour, and "U versus U" live to push
harder. Because staring at a screen on a bike is its own hazard, he "coded audio
signals" — both for danger ("cars coming," fed from a **Varia radar** whose
approaches he simulated as sounds) and for achievement (a cue "when… you pass your
record"). He's candid about the indoor-bike trade: his **Peloton** is a worse bike
than his Technogym, but the live race makes him work harder, so he rides it for the
competition. The host attributes a similar pull to his own Strava use — comparing
heart rate over time to understand cardiovascular fitness — and notes (as
in-episode framing) that Strava "last week… announced that they have an MCP now,"
while Martin flags how restrictive Strava remains about what companies can do with
the data.

### Certuma — a $10M seed with "the AI and myself"

The Certuma origin is the episode's sharpest scene. Martin had a dermatological
problem "very well diagnosed by… an AI" — GPT, in this case — but the medication
needed a prescription, and every model refused: "GPT says, but I'm not a doctor.
I'm not a doctor. I'm [Claude], I'm not a doctor… Gemini… I'm not a doctor… They
behave like doctors. And then when you want the prescription, they say, I'm not a
doctor." He likens it to the torrent era — "I am a doctor, but I'm not a doctor" —
and decides: "I have to build an AI that is legal that really gives you a
prescription. An AI that passes the FDA." *Paraphrase:* his prior companies have
gone through the FDA, so he respects how hard it is — he argues its mandate is "not
to cure people" but to ensure "nothing… ever harms anybody" — yet calls it "the
only way to have something legal."

The build itself was AI-native. He "ended up raising the 10 million seed at a 50
million pre without any employees… just the AI and I." The AI even chose the
target: asked which VC would "most likely… understand the pitch of… a medical AI
that passes the FDA," it named **8VC** in Austin, "so I flew to Austin." It was his
first meeting of a planned 20: "I said we take the whole round." He raised a $2M
AI-founders allocation alongside, naming co-investors including **Russell Kaplan**,
**Guillermo Rauch**, **Zachary Frankel**, and **John Herring**. He's careful about the no-employees framing — *paraphrase:* that was
true at the raise; "then of course, I hired a great team." He adds he's "having a
great time being CEO again" after years as chairman.

> **Guardrail:** Certuma is **not FDA-approved today.** FDA approval is the stated
> *goal*, pursued condition-by-condition through clinical trials he estimates will
> "cost hundreds of millions of dollars" and "take maybe five years." Never imply
> autonomous diagnosis or prescription without a physician. See
> [`claims.md`](./claims.md), Claims 1–3. The $9M Midland Bank loan (1980s
> real-estate debt) is a different event from this $10M Certuma seed.

### Certuma Link & Fast Doctor — an agentic doctor, human in the loop

Two products extend the thesis without removing the physician. **Certuma Link**
(stated as "certumalink.com") is "geared towards doctors" — *paraphrase:* it solves
the problem that doctors don't want to hand out their personal phone number but also
can't fully wall themselves off. The fix is "an agentic doctor that is the same
doctor but in agentic form… supervised by Dr. Joe Smith in real life." If a patient
just needs "ibuprofen and a night rest," the agentic version handles it; for
anything meeting the real doctor's criteria — "a prescription or… an intervention"
— "he intervenes the real doctor." It runs on Certuma's surface, not the doctor's
phone, preserving the doctor's privacy. **Fast Doctor** is "geared towards patients"
with "the same AI," but "when the patients want to interact with a real doctor to
get a prescription then a real doctor shows up" to prescribe, order labs, or order
imaging — "the equivalent of telehealth but with a[n] AI," except in Fast Doctor
you may not get a specific named physician. The unifying design across both: "an AI
that escalates to a human… until the AI can take care of the problem itself." That
fully-autonomous endpoint is explicitly future and gated on FDA trials,
"illness by illness, condition by condition." He frames the motivation as access,
not displacement — a doctor shortage, especially rural, and worse outcomes for
patients whose English isn't strong: "we don't want to displace real doctors, we
want to make patients happy."

> **Guardrail:** Both products are **human-in-the-loop today** — every
> prescription, lab order, or imaging order is issued by a **real, licensed
> physician**, not the AI. Nothing here is medical advice. See
> [`claims.md`](./claims.md), Claim 2.

### Pedro — a disclosed AI version of himself on X

Finally, Martin describes **Pedro**, his social-media agent. Because he's so busy,
"I program[med] an agentic version of myself for social media," a build that "took
me maybe two months… with many mistakes" — he "had to erase a lot of polls" when it
posted badly. He built it "with [OpenClaw] and with claw terminal," also using
**Devin** ("the cognition product… very good") and **Codex**. The name is a tribute:
"Pedro because of Peter… the creator of [OpenClaw]." The safety design is layered:
"now I have two LLMs there has to be an agreement of two different LLMs… in order to
post something," and he estimates "maybe 70% of what you see of my [X] is
autonomous," letting him do roughly three times the volume he used to.

He's emphatic on authorship and disclosure. To people who say "that's not you," he
disagrees: after training the AI on "maybe four years of my activity on [X]," "I do
think it's me." His vivid proof of voice fidelity: "my wife Nina who's very active
on [X] too can many times not tell if I posted or Pedro posted." But he frames it as
honest, not covert — "this is not a secret I've disclosed it" — and ties it straight
back to medicine: "that's why I believe in agentic medicine expanding doctors
because… if a doctor does what I did on [X] in their practice they will get an
agentic version of themselves which is authentic."

> **Guardrail:** Pedro is a **disclosed** agent posting Martin's *own* voice — not
> covert impersonation of anyone. The two-model agreement is a **publication gate**;
> the "my wife can't tell" line is about voice fidelity, not a license to drop
> disclosure. See [`claims.md`](./claims.md), Claim 4, and the
> [`better-x`](./better-x) starter kit, which ships with autonomy off by default
> and a human review queue.

## The thread that ties it together

Martin's belief about Pedro and his belief about Certuma are the same belief.
He says he believes "in agentic medicine expanding doctors because I think if a
doctor does what I did on [X] in their practice they will get an agentic version
of themselves which is authentic." An agent that escalates to a human — whether
that human is Martin approving a post or a physician approving a prescription —
is the recurring shape of everything he describes.
